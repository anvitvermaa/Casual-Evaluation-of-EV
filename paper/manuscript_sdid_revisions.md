## Methodology Section Revisions

### Synthetic Difference-in-Differences (SDiD)
To estimate the causal impact of the Maharashtra EV Policy 2025, we employ the Synthetic Difference-in-Differences (SDiD) estimator developed by Arkhangelsky et al. (2021). SDiD resolves two fundamental limitations of standard Synthetic Control Methods (SCM) when applied to small, heterogeneous macroeconomic panels. 

First, standard SCM requires the treated unit to reside strictly within the convex hull of the donor pool—a rigid geometric constraint that forces the synthetic counterfactual to be a strictly positive interpolation of donor states ($\sum w_j = 1, w_j \geq 0$). Given that Maharashtra is an economic outlier in terms of both GSDP and industrial scale, this constraint forces standard optimizers into boundary solutions that bias pre-treatment fit. SDiD mathematically relaxes this restriction by incorporating unit fixed effects ($\alpha_i$) and time fixed effects ($\beta_t$), introducing an intercept shift that allows the synthetic counterfactual to extrapolate parallel trends vertically outside the bounds of the donor pool's convex hull.

Second, SDiD applies L2 ridge regularization ($\zeta^2 T_0 \|\omega\|^2$) to the calculation of donor unit weights ($\omega_i$). Standard SCM frequently results in fragile estimators dominated by a single donor state, creating critical vulnerabilities if that single "anchor" experiences an unobserved idiosyncratic shock. By penalizing extreme weight concentration, SDiD enforces empirical dispersion across the donor pool, yielding a structurally resilient synthetic control. Furthermore, time weights ($\lambda_t$) are applied to selectively up-weight pre-treatment periods that possess cross-sectional profiles predictive of the post-treatment era, mitigating Omitted Variable Bias (OVB) inherent in matching strictly on distant, irrelevant lagged outcomes. To further strengthen identification, we explicitly integrate `GSDP_per_capita` and `Urbanization_Rate` as covariates via the optimizer's covariate-augmented matrix design (Arkhangelsky et al., 2021, Eq. 4.1).

## Limitations Section Revisions

### Statistical Power in Macro-Panels
The primary limitation of this study lies in the statistical constraints inherent to sub-national macroeconomic panels. Our donor pool is restricted to $J=8$ highly comparable Indian states. In a traditional SCM permutation framework, a panel of $N=9$ total units yields a minimum mathematically achievable pseudo p-value of $1/9 \approx 0.111$, rendering conventional thresholds of statistical significance ($\alpha = 0.05$) structurally impossible to detect regardless of the true effect size. We circumvent this fatal constraint by utilizing SDiD's non-parametric placebo standard errors, which provide valid inference and construct genuine confidence intervals independent of $N$.

However, the resulting wide confidence intervals associated with our Average Treatment Effect ($ATT$) must be interpreted through the lens of statistical power (Type II error). While our SDiD estimation isolates the causal policy effect around a null magnitude (a near-zero ATT), the width of the confidence interval indicates that the data lack the necessary statistical power to definitively rule out moderate positive or negative policy effects. Therefore, rather than claiming definitive proof that the Maharashtra EV Policy 2025 exerted exactly *zero* influence on the market, we must frame this finding as a failure to reject the null hypothesis amidst significant national macroeconomic noise (such as the expiration of the FAME-II federal subsidy). Ex-post evaluations must confront the reality that detecting isolated policy signals against turbulent national baselines requires extended post-treatment windows—our 13-month horizon serves as vital preliminary evidence, but long-term longitudinal data (24-36 months) remains strictly necessary to fully untangle the causal displacement mechanisms at play.
